Sundial

From wikipedia, the free encyclopedia.

A sundial measures time designs by the position of the sun. The most commonly seen design such as
'odinary' or standard garden sundial, casts shadow on a flat surface marked with the hours of
the day. As the position of the sun changes, the time indicated by the shadow changes. However,
the sundials can be designed for any surface where a fixed object casts a predictable shadow.

Most sundials design indicate apperant solar time. Minor design veriations can measure standard
and daylight saving time, as well.

History

Sundial in the form of obelisks (3500 BC) are known from ancient
Egypt, and were developed further by other cultures, including the Chinese, Greek and Roman
culture. A type of sundial without goomen is described in the old Old Testament(Isaiah 38:2).

The mathematician and astronomer Theodosrus of Bithynia (Ca, 160 BC-Ca, 100 BC) is said to have
invented a universal sundial that could have anywhere on Earth. The French astronomer Orance
Fine constructed a sundial of ivory in 1524. The Italian astronomer Giovano Padovani pubblishe a
treaties on the sundial 1570, in which he included instructions for the manufacture and laying out of
mural (vertical) and horizental sundials. Gluseppe Biancani's construction instrumenti ad horologia
solaria discussed how to make a perfect sundial, with accompanying illustrations.
